Projects

Fungal Fiction

As part of the English Honors Program, Gracie completed an undergraduate thesis paper in her penultimate semester. For this project, she extensively researched both literary and scientific fields. Her 40-page essay, titled “Fungal Fiction: The Development of Fungi in Literature from the Nineteenth to Twenty-First Centuries,” engaged with an abundance of scholarship, three primary sources, and a unique thesis. A presentation of her paper was accepted into the College of Humanities and Social Science's 2024 Undergraduate Research Symposium and was presented the Innovation in Methods and Ideas Award.

Abstract: Fungi are liminal, boundary-defying organisms in the spheres of science, culture, and literature. Scholars conclude that fungi, with their scientific and cultural (mis)understandings, are employed in fiction as a method of exploring the Anthropocene, posthumanism, and alternative human-nonhuman relations. However, literary fungi are not a common topic of scholarship, and even less so is a mapping of their development across time. By crafting a literary timeline from the nineteenth to twenty-first century and performing close readings of touchstone texts, as well as relating their portrayal to (ethno)mycological understandings across the centuries, I demonstrate the continuities and disparities of fungi in literature. This essay further illuminates the non-normative literary forms found in fungal fiction and the interconnections of the sciences and humanities. My analysis reveals that fungal fiction acts as a method for humans to engage with complex aspects of the environment that queer the distinction between human and nonhuman. Until the mid-twentieth century, these human-environment entanglements were expressions of fear concerned with maintaining the human as separate from or above nature. Moving toward the turn of the twenty-first century, however, there was a shift toward imagining a posthuman, multispecies identity through fungal engagements. I argue that this timeline illuminates our changing perception and interaction with the Anthropocene and that fungal fiction itself acts as its own multispecies entanglement between humans and fungi.
